Statistics and Sampling Variability

  • Statistic: A quantity computed from values in a sample.
    • Given a sample from a population, the sample mean, sample proportion, or sample standard deviation computed from this sample are examples of statistic.
  • Sampling Variability: The observed value of a statistic depends on the particular sample selected from the population and it will vary from sample to sample. This variability is called sampling variability.
  • Sampling Distribution: A sampling distribution is a probability distribution of a certain statistic based on many random samples from a single population.
    • Sampling distribution of sample mean, sample proportion, sample standard deviation.

Properties of the Sampling Distribution of the Mean

  • The following are properties of the sample means when they are computed from randomly selected samples.
    1. The average of the sample means is the true (population) mean.
    2. The standard deviation of the sample means is the true (population) standard deviation divided by the square root of the sample size.
    3. If the original population is normal, the sampling distribution of the sample mean is also normal.
    4. Central Limit Theorem: If the sample size n is sufficiently large, the sampling distribution of the sample mean can be approximated by the normal distribution.

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

  • Example: We observed clearly that our original population of Annual salary is not normally distributed.
  • As a result of the CLT, we can perform normal probability calculations involving the sample mean salary when the sample size is sufficiently large.
  • The usual cut-off for a large sample size is \(n=30\).
  • When a sample of 50 CEO’s is randomly selected from the data ceo_salary, we can compute the following:
      1. probability that the sample mean salary is below \(\$11\) Mil.
      1. probability that the sample mean salary is between \(\$10\) Mil and \(\$11\) Mil.
pnorm(q = 11, mean = mu, sd = sigma/sqrt(50))
## [1] 0.6118795
  • The probability that the sample mean salary is below \(\$11\) Mil is \(0.6119\)
pnorm(q = 11, mean = mu, sd = sigma/sqrt(50)) - pnorm(q = 10, mean = mu, sd = sigma/sqrt(50))
## [1] 0.2420846
  • The probability that the sample mean salary is between \(\$10\) Mil and \(\$11\) Mil is \(0.2421\)

Sampling Distribution of Sample Proportion.

  • Just like sample means, we can compute the sample proportions, visualize the distribution of the sample proportions and make some calculations.
  • The distribution of sample proportions is known as the sampling distribution of the proportion and has the following properties:
    • \(\mu_p = P\); where \(p\) is sample proportion and \(P\) is population proportion.
    • \(\sigma_p = \sqrt{\frac{P(1-P)}{n}}\); where \(P\) is population proportion and \(n\) is the sample size.
  • The properties stated above are valid if the sampling distribution is approximately normal.
  • The sampling distribution of a sample proportion is approximately normal if the expected number of successes and failures are both at least 10. That is \(n*P \geq 10\) and \(n*(1-P) \geq 10\).

Sampling Distribution of Sample Standard Deviation

  • Again, we can compute sample standard deviations and visualize the distribution of the sampling distributions.
  • In general, when samples of size \(n\) are taken from a normal distribution with population variance \(\sigma^2\) , the sampling distribution of the \((n-1)S^2/ \sigma^2\) has a chi-square distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom.
  • We can then compute probabilities using qchisq().
draws = matrix(rnorm(1000 * 6, 0, 3), 6)
drawvar = apply(draws, 2, var)


draws = 5 * drawvar/9
hist(draws, breaks = 20, prob = TRUE, main = "standard distribution for sample variance")
v = seq(0, max(draws), length = 200)
lines(v, dchisq(v, 5), lty = 2, lwd = 2)

Deleting outliers

  • Using the interquartile range to find outliers
    1. Sort your data from low to high
    2. Identify the median, the first quartile (Q1), and the third quartile (Q3)
    3. Calculate your IQR. IQR = Q3 - Q1
    4. Calculate your upper fence. UF = Q3 + 1.5*IQR
    5. Calculate your lower fence. LF = Q1 - 1.5*IQR
    6. Use your fences to highlight any outliers, all values that fall outside your fences.
# Deleting outliers
#find Q1, Q3, and interquartile range for values in column A
Q1 <- quantile(ceo_salary$Annual.pay, .25)
Q3 <- quantile(ceo_salary$Annual.pay, .75)
IQR <- IQR(ceo_salary$Annual.pay)
boxplot(ceo_salary$Annual.pay,ylim=c(0,(Q3 + 1.5*IQR)))

#only keep rows in dataframe that have values within 1.5*IQR of Q1 and Q3
ceo_salary_no_outliers <- subset(ceo_salary, ceo_salary$Annual.pay > (Q1 - 1.5*IQR) 
                                 & ceo_salary$Annual.pay < (Q3 + 1.5*IQR))

hist(ceo_salary_no_outliers$Annual.pay)
